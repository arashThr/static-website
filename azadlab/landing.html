A practical question

Know what you own.

  • Your photos and memories
    May be used to train AI models. Your account can be locked with no support channel that reaches a real person. You're renting access to your own archive.
  • Your messages and conversations
    Scanned for content, used to build profiles. End-to-end encryption is the exception. The question is not paranoia — it's which tools choose privacy by design.
  • Your browsing and habits
    Build a detailed picture of who you are. In most cases this shapes ads. In some, it shapes something more consequential.
  • Your tools and creative work
    Rented monthly. Stop paying and lose access. Locked into formats or platforms you don't control — until you decide otherwise.
  • Your connectivity
    You rent internet access too. It can be throttled, disrupted, or switched off. Owning what runs locally changes what you can do when it is.

This workshop is about changing the ratio. Not abandoning tools that bring real value — but knowing which ones you own versus which ones you borrow.

The workshops

5 sessions.
Real tools.
Yours to keep.

Every session ends with something running on your own machine - something you own, control, and that keeps working offline. No subscriptions. No corporate accounts. No single point of failure.

SESSION 01
Your Computer, Your Rules
Everything you do on Mac or Windows, you can do on Linux — often better, and often more beautifully. This session is about seeing what your computer can be when it works for you.
  • Linux can look exactly how you want — demo of modern desktops
  • Browse, code, write, design — the full workflow is here
  • No Copilot watching what you type; no forced updates; no walled garden
  • Install any software without asking an app store for permission
  • The terminal as a power tool, not a scary thing
You leave with:
A working Linux install that feels like home.
Ubuntu · Fedora · bash · apt/dnf
SESSION 02
Your Photos, Your Files, Your Server
Your uploaded photos may be training AI models you'll never see. And when a corporate account gets locked — with no support, no appeal — your memories go with it. This session puts them back in your hands.
  • What platforms do with uploaded photos (AI training, content analysis)
  • The reality of account lockouts: no Google support exists for individuals
  • Docker: the concept that makes self-hosting accessible
  • Immich: your private photo library — live and working by end of session
  • Tailscale: access everything from your phone, privately, anywhere
  • Hardware options: old laptop, Raspberry Pi, cheap European VPS
You leave with:
Immich running and syncing from your phone. Jellyfin setup as homework.
Docker · Immich · Jellyfin · Tailscale · Caddy
SESSION 03
Your Own Little Server
A $50 device can block ads across your whole network, automate your home, and run your own services — no subscription, no cloud account, no third party in the loop.
  • Raspberry Pi: what it is, what it can do, first boot
  • Pi-hole: network-wide ad and tracker blocking — covers every device at home
  • Home Assistant: open-source home automation, fully offline
  • Lights, sensors, climate — without Amazon or Google involved
  • The bigger idea: once you can run a Pi, you can run anything
You leave with:
Pi-hole blocking ads on your whole network. Home Assistant installed and controlling one thing.
Raspberry Pi · Pi-hole · Home Assistant
SESSION 04
AI: Trust & Own It
Every query you send to ChatGPT or Copilot goes to a cloud server. Your questions reveal your work, your thinking, your politics. But AI can now run entirely on your own machine — on the same laptop you're holding.
  • What LLMs actually are — demystified, no maths
  • What your queries reveal and where they go when you send them
  • Ollama: one command to run any open model locally
  • Gemma 4 (by Google) and other open models — quality is there
  • LM Studio: a GUI for running local AI, no command line required
  • Practical uses: chat, document Q&A, coding help — all offline
You leave with:
Ollama running with Gemma 4. A local AI assistant on your own machine.
Ollama · Gemma 4 · LM Studio
SESSION 05
When the Network Goes Dark
Power cut. ISP down. Infrastructure attack. What tools still work? This session is about communicating and staying coordinated when the internet isn't available.
  • BitChat: mesh messaging over Bluetooth and WiFi — no internet required
  • Meshtastic + LoRa radio: long-range mesh network without any infrastructure
  • Syncthing: peer-to-peer file sync, no servers involved
  • The Nomad project: a framework for the most essential digital emergency items
  • Lessons from Iran, Ukraine, and Sudan — what actually worked offline
You leave with:
BitChat installed and tested. A personal offline resilience checklist.
BitChat · Meshtastic · LoRa · Syncthing

You don't need
to be technical.
You need to decide
what's yours.

Every workshop is designed for complete beginners. We explain what we're doing and why - in plain language, without jargon.

Whether you're a developer or have never touched a terminal, these sessions are a good opportunity to set up your safety nets together, in a group. The technical people sharpen their skills. The non-technical people discover the tools aren't as intimidating as they look. Everyone leaves with something running.

Sweden already understands preparedness. MSB asks every household to stock food and water for 72 hours. Digital resilience is the same instinct applied to the tools you depend on every single day - and it starts here.

I'm Arash - a Stockholm-based software engineer with more than a decade of professional experience, and a relationship with open source that goes back much further than that.

My first Linux installations were Ubuntu 5.10 and Fedora 4. Watching Revolution OS changed how I thought about software - not just as a tool, but as a question about who controls the infrastructure you depend on. I became an active member of the Linux User Group in Iran, and I've been building on that foundation ever since.

Nothing made the stakes clearer than growing up Iranian. I saw firsthand how critical the free flow of information is - and how quickly it can be taken away from an entire population. That experience is the foundation of this curriculum.
